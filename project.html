Bargaining Strength of Employees in the UK

Project Aim

Many economists believe wage growth is positively correlated with the job vacancy ratio and negatively correlated with unemployment rate. The extent of labour market tightness determines the bargaining strength of employees in wage negotiations with firms, and subsequently the negotiated wage. I want to investigate the extent to which this widely-accepted theory holds. I establish the relationship between wage growth and the unemployment and job vacancy rate by studying the relationship between the job vacancy ratio and real wage growth for the UK, before analysing different UK sectors.

Data Description

Inflation (Chart 3) and Unemployment (Chart 4) data was dowloaded from their API links using loops in Google Colab. For other charts, data was also downloaded from their API links. However, instead of directly using the APIs the data values for each year in January were manually typed onto excel and saved in CSV format. Although Chart 1 analyses real wage growth, this data couldn't be found for individual sectors, hence nominal growth was used to study the influence of bargaining power from vacancy ratio fluctuations. The use of APIs is justified because charts 3 and 4 can update automatically. However, APIs were not directly used in the other charts because I wanted to present annual trends given the purpose of the project is to go as far back in time as available in the ONS database. The Covid-19 Pandemic is included as it demonstrates the strength of employee bargaining power during a period that saw record job vacancy ratios. All data was uploaded onto my GitHub account with the naem "project" included in each relevant json file for clarity. The data source is the ONS.

Conclusion Part 1: UK Trends Key Results

Real Wage = Nominal Wage - (Nominal Wage X Inflaition Rate). The vancacy ratio is correlated with Real Wage growth. The latter plummeted from 4.8% in 2007 to -4% between 2007-2009, with the Vacancy Ratio simultaneously decreasing by 29%. Post financial crisis, there's a positive relationship between the two variables until the pandemic commenced, with real wage growth improving by 2.3 percentage points and the vacancy ratio increasing from 1.7-2.7 per 100 employee jobs from 2010-2020. This is suggestive of improvements to employee bargaining power, however there were considerable fluctuations to real wage growth in this period. Inflation trends in the 2010s can capture nominal wage chnages by applying the real wage equation. Inflation dropped from 4.7% in 2011Q3 to 0.1% in 2015Q1, indicating falling inflation contributed to real wage growth. However, in the latter part of the decade it rose to 3% by 2017Q4, which may have suppressed real wage growth. What is interesting about the pandemic is the vacancy rate reaching a record 4.1, although real wage growth decreased by 3.4 percentage points in 2022. Given inflation reached a record 10% in 2022Q3, the real wage growth could be attributed to diminishing employee purchasing power. Post-financial crisis, the unemployment rate fell dramtically, halving between 2010Q1 and 2020Q1. This trend suggests bargaining power improved because frictional unemployment is more likely to be lower. What's less clear is how real wage growth still increases between 2020-2021 despite unemployment increasing from 4% to 5.2% between 2020Q1-2020Q4.

Conclusion Part 2: UK sectors

Nominal wage growth and vacancy ratio trends for construction adheres to classical economic theory. Here, there's a time gap between vacancy ratio chnages and wage growth. Between 2007-2008, the vacancy ratio fell by 62.5% and wage growth stagnated between 2011-2013 with average growth of -0.7%. Wholesailing recorded fluctuations in wage growth, even though vacancy ratios were less volatile pre-pandemic. Regarding Finance, the period 2004-2007 sees consistent high wage growth averaging 6.1%, whereas the period 2012-2015 sees growth stagnate at -0.6%. Whilst the vacancy ratio does increase from 3.1-3.7/100 in the former period, vacancy growth is unexpectedly higher in the latter period.

Project Takeaways

Without increasing sector samples, it's difficult to analyse the strength of employee bargaining power for all sectors. Further, research only covers the UK, hence additional work in the same sectors for other developed economies would be required to study international differences in the relationship between vacancy ratio and wage growth. Data used for the UK as a whole indicates that increases in vacancy rates does lead to wage growth increases, albeit with certain exceptions, suggesting employees leverage greater bargaining power to negotiate wage increases. With more vacancies available, employees are confident of finding employment elsewhere. individual sectors don't always adhere to classical economic theory. Some sectors record time lags, whereas there's no clear observable relationship for others.
